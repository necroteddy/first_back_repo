That Email Looks Real.
It Is Not.

Phishing emails trick millions of people every year. Here's how to spot one before it's too late.

FROM: security@paypa1.com ← fake domain!
TO: you@email.com
SUBJ: ⚠ Urgent: Your account has been suspended
Dear Valued Customer,

We have detected suspicious activity on your account. Your account will be permanently closed in 24 hours unless you verify your information immediately.

Verify My Account Now

PayPal Security Team — Do not reply to this email.

🚩 RED FLAG: "paypa1.com" — the L is a number 1
🚩 RED FLAG: Creates panic with "24 hours" deadline
🚩 RED FLAG: Asks you to click a link to "verify"

That email above is a phishing scam. It looks like it came from PayPal. It did not. A hacker made it to steal your password. Millions of people fall for emails like this every day.

"Phishing works because it doesn't attack your computer. It attacks your emotions — fear, urgency, trust."

Hackers send fake emails that look real. They pretend to be your bank, Amazon, Netflix, or even your boss. The goal is always the same — get you to click a link or type your password.

Here is how to spot a phishing email:

  • [01]Check the sender's email address. Look carefully. "paypa1.com" is not "paypal.com". One wrong letter = fake.
  • [02]Watch for urgency. "Act now!" "24 hours!" "Your account is locked!" — this is pressure to make you panic and not think.
  • [03]Hover over links before clicking. The real URL appears at the bottom of your screen. If it looks strange — do not click.
  • [04]Real companies never ask for your password by email. Ever. If they do — it is fake.
  • [05]When in doubt, go directly to the website. Open a new tab. Type the address yourself. Do not use the link in the email.
// What to do if you clicked

Don't panic. Change your password immediately on that site. Turn on two-factor authentication. Then check your other accounts — especially if you reuse passwords.

Phishing is the most common cyber attack in the world. But now you know what to look for. Slow down. Read carefully. Trust your gut. If something feels wrong — it probably is.
