Why passwords matter

Passwords are the keys to your digital life. They protect your email, bank, photos, and more. But most people use weak passwords — and hackers know it.

Hacker software can guess millions of passwords per second. A simple password is cracked instantly. A strong one takes centuries.

According to the Verizon Data Breach Report, over 80% of hacking-related breaches involve weak or stolen passwords.

💪

What makes a strong password?

A strong password has four qualities:

  • ✓Long — at least 12 characters, ideally 16 or more
  • ✓Random — not your name, birthday, or a real word
  • ✓Mixed — letters, numbers, and symbols combined
  • ✓Unique — never reused on different websites
sunshine1 Weak — cracked in under 1 second
T#9mQ!xzL2@vP Strong — centuries to crack

Try a passphrase: Four random words joined together — like correct-horse-battery-staple. Long and easier to remember.

🚫

Never reuse passwords

Using the same password everywhere is dangerous. If one site gets hacked, criminals try that same password on your email, bank, and shopping accounts.

This is called credential stuffing — and it works millions of times a day.

Check now: See if your email was already stolen at HaveIBeenPwned.com — a free, trusted tool used by security professionals.

🗄️

Use a password manager

A password manager stores all your passwords safely. You only remember one master password. It creates and fills in strong, unique passwords for every site automatically.

The National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) recommends password managers as a best practice for everyone.

↗ Bitwarden Free and open-source. Great for everyone. ↗ 1Password Popular, polished, easy to use. ↗ Dashlane Good choice for beginners.
📱

Turn on two-factor authentication

Two-factor authentication (2FA) adds a second step when you log in — like a code sent to your phone. Even if someone steals your password, they still can't get in without that second code.

Enable 2FA on your most important accounts: email, banking, and social media. Find out how at TwoFactorAuth.org.

🎣

Watch out for phishing

Hackers trick people into typing passwords into fake websites that look real — like a copy of your bank's login page.

Always check the web address before entering a password. Look for https:// and a padlock icon.

Safe habit: If you get an email asking you to log in somewhere, go directly to the website yourself instead of clicking the link.

🔄

When to change your password

You do not need to change passwords every 30 days. The FTC recommends focusing on strength and uniqueness instead. Change a password if:

  • !A website you use was hacked
  • !You shared a password with someone
  • !You logged in on a public or shared computer
